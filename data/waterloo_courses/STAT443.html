Course Description

Calendar Description for STAT 443

Modelling techniques for forecasting time series data: smoothing methods, regression including penalty/regularization methods, the Box-Jenkins framework, stationary and non-stationary processes, both with and without seasonal effects. Other topics may include: ARCH/GARCH models, Bayesian methods, dynamic linear models, Markov Chain Monte Carlo simulation, spectral density analysis, and periodograms.

View requirements for STAT 443

This course introduces the fundamentals of statistical forecasting, with a focus on how empirical data and appropriate modelling can be used to generate forecasts and assess uncertainty surrounding forecasts. Emphasis is placed on both the theoretical development of forecasting models and their practical applications. The course focuses more on time series analysis and modelling, building on students’ prior knowledge of regression. Topics include linear regression and regularized models for forecasting, exponential smoothing and the Holt–Winters algorithm, as well as the Box–Jenkins approach. Time permitting, we may also introduce the Kalman filter and volatility models such as ARCH/GARCH. Applications will be drawn from a wide range of fields. A solid background in regression analysis (e.g., STAT 331, 371, SYDE 334, or an equivalent course) is essential for this course.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Be able to give a qualitative description of the structures of various types of time series, and discuss, through descriptive approaches, the following topics: time scales, trends, variability, seasonality, outliers, change points, and stationarity.
Be able to select, fit, and critique forecasting models for a given dataset, and to use such models to make forecasts.
Be able to quantify the uncertainties and check the underlying assumptions associated with each of the forecasting and filtering statistical methodologies studied in the class, and to understand and comment on the limitations of each model.
Be able to discuss, with a variety of examples, the importance of forecasting in real-world applications and how to quantify the efficacy of different methods. Students will also be able to discuss fundamental differences between short, medium and long term prediction.
Be able to use R-based tools to generate appropriate plots and summary statistics to communicate to others important aspects of various types of time series, and forecasting problems.
Be able to use R-based tools to carry out forecasting analyses and to understand the R output of a forecasting study.

Late / Missed Content

Late Submission Policy: A 25% penalty is applied per day for late submissions. The minimum late submission penalty is 25%. These penalties are for the entire paper, starting immediately after Crowdmark's deadline.  Please be aware that although you can make as many submissions as you wish before the deadline, Crowdmark will only accept one submission after the deadline has passed (and this is only if you have not submitted anything at all before the deadline). If you have already submitted any portion of the assignment before the deadline, you cannot submit and/or change any portion of your responses after the deadline. If you do have to submit an assignment late, ensure that your submission is complete by uploading all your files and then clicking the "Submit" button. If you start uploading a file a few minutes before the deadline, but the submission process completes after the deadline, you will be penalized for late submission. Do not leave your submission until the last minute. If, for any reason, technical or otherwise (e.g. last-minute internet outage, travelling, etc.), you email your assignment to the instructor to submit for you, there will be a 15% per-day penalty additional to the late submission penalty measured from the time-stamp of your email. All formatting rules to be outlined in each assignment apply to email submissions, i.e. email submissions that are not one PDF file per part per question will be ignored.

Missed Tutorial Test Policy: A maximum of one missed Tutorial Test may be accommodated if there is a valid reason. In such cases, the score of zero for the missed test will be replaced by the percentile-matched score based on the Term Test. For example, if your Term Test score falls at the 75th percentile, and you missed Tutorial Test 2, your score for that test will be set to the 75th percentile of the Tutorial Test 2 grade distribution. This method ensures that students who miss a test are neither unfairly advantaged nor disadvantaged, particularly if the Term Test is significantly easier or harder than the missed tutorial test. Please note that this grade imputation policy applies only to students who miss one tutorial test. If you miss two or more tutorial tests, you will either fail the course or be considered for an INC (incomplete) grade, depending on the circumstances and your overall performance in the course.

Missed Assignment Policy: If you fail to submit an assignment and have a valid reason with supporting documentation, the weight from that assignment will be shifted to the relevant tutorial tests and/or the term test. In the case of a 2-day self-declared absence (see below), a 2-day extension will be provided.

Important Note Regarding Missed Assessments: If you need to miss a test or assignment, or if you are requesting a 2-day extension for an assignment under UW policy, you must:

  1. Report your absence/extension request on Quest (two-day extension, absence, VIF submission)

  2. Complete this MS Form within 48 hours of the missed deadline. The form will automatically send an email to the instructor on your behalf.

Accommodations will be considered only if both of these steps are completed.

Generative AI

You may use generative AI tools for the following purposes only if disclosed in your submission:

  • Grammar/spell-checking or rewording for clarity (e.g., Grammarly, or similar features in ChatGPT)

  • Debugging or explaining small code snippets to aid understanding (not for full code generation)

  • Brainstorming ideas or outlining structure, as long as the content is not copied verbatim

If you choose to use AI in this limited way, you must include an “AI Use Appendix” with your assignment and/or project. For each instance of AI use, you must specify:

  • What tool(s) you used

  • What input(s) or prompt(s) you provided

  • What output(s) you received

  • How you incorporated or modified the output in your final work

Failure to disclose AI use, or using it in ways that violate this policy, will be considered a breach of academic integrity and will be addressed in accordance with the University of Waterloo’s academic integrity policy (see below).
